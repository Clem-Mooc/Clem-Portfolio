Frank Ocean Infographic

Applied visual information design principles such as:
Visual potential, Visual language, Visual hierarchy, Use of color (TEACH)

To create an infographic (via Adobe Illustrator) that effectively conveys information about Grammy-award winning singer, song-writer Frank Ocean, within the 5 principles of Visual Potential:
Appearance, Relation, Numbers, Time, and Location.

Appearance

Exploiting the visual potential of appearance

First Draft

  • Option 1: A highly detailed photo of Frank is posted in a similar fashion to style guide. This will display how Frank looks like, and some basic stats about him.
  • Option 2: A simplified, yet realistic outline of Frank Ocean. Clusters together a couple of basic Frank Ocean stats. This would ideally be placed in the large timeline swirl. Teaches less about what Frank Ocean looks like, but the bold, red lettering of Frank Ocean does discuss what topic the page is about.
    • In terms of the visual style guide, I match Appearance by applying the same tan-colored theme. This circular shape with Franks' head in a sepia filter dictates him as part of history. Within the same proximity of other heads, this has potential for producing effective relation hierarchies. Lastly, the text is left aligned, and has a clear hierarchy of header and body text, emphasized with an underline in between. Appearance tries to match with Bauhaus logo. In addition, numbers and facts around the side provide Number visual language. The font is also in Futura like the infographic's font, and as a san serif typeface, retains the simplicity of Bauhaus.
    The colors emulate the sepia toned feel of the other pictures. This vintage-feeling photo can help evoke thoughts as if Frank Ocean is a timeless artist for this generation. The colors express ubiquity and accessibility, and Frank Ocean possesses both. At the same time, the reds around him will define the avant-garde, maverick themes that Frank is associated with. The colors are emulated 1:1.

    First Revision

    Despite asking for critiques on the version 1, my critics couldn't find many criticisms for it. Thus, I decided to go with the #2. With the intent of having this be the centerpiece, this logo must clearly indicate that Frank Ocean is the topic of this infographic, while also retain simplified details. I will provide some complementary text above the swirls to increase information as i develop the infographic.
    I did significant revisions, including removing outlines completely, and adding color fills to blank areas. I also added key colors, such as the green hair to represent Frank Ocean's most recognizable form, and colored in his beard to help distinguish his face. The point of the colors was that while the photo has fewer details, the logo retains the Frank Ocean look as the colors can bring up associations with Frank Ocean from his latest studio album "Blonde".

    In terms of the visual style guide. I did a good attempt at following it. I was able to emulate the swirls, but also put my own creative spin, replacing the swirl to a vinyl record with a tonearm needle. this still retains the abstract, simple style, with simple shapes, and solid colors. The font will be Futura Bold, and is a perfect font to follow as Frank Ocean references Futura in his song "Futura Free". Frank is also a devout fan of design, and I can see many of his aesthetical inspirations coming from Bauhaus, so being able to tie this together is a meaningful, symbolic combination.
    I tried to experiment with some slightly different color combinations, while keeping some of the colors from the original color guide. I wanted to get a background color that complemented the lime green of Frank Ocean's hair, so I tried out a specific hue of blue, which was actually pulled from one of Frank Ocean common color schemes used in promotional materials. Apart from that, I aim to follow very closely to the color guide.

    • Location

    Exploiting the visual potential of location

    First Draft

    Views should instantly recognize the dotted backdrop as a dotted world map. The viewers will also see the pictures of various studios, with a header that says "Studios" on the top left. The pictures have annotations that show the album that was worked on in the studio, studio name, and studio location. Leader lines point up to the area that the studio is located in, with an outlined endpoint to mark the spot.
    While each of the pictures have different sizes, their annotations are all the same font size. The difference in picture size is merely the aspect ratio of the pictures, and so the text will help to show that each of these locations are equally weighted and have no distinguishing hierarchy. The sepia overlay is also used to describe some form of uniformity in that they are all studios. Leading lines were created by moving studios in a way that wouldn't make the line clip into the pictures. The white lines complement the white text, and mix together with the annotations, to show a direct linear relationship between the location to the location at the other end of the line.

    Relationships

    Exploiting the visual potential of relationships

    First Draft

    Graphic takeaway
    For Option #1, I learned how many different genres and subgenres Frank Ocean has performed in his music. We see the primary genres, or generally just genres, such as hip hop, rock, r&b, etc. and those are then color-coded and subdivided into alternative, classic, contemporary, etc forms of the primary genre. This extends out to more obscure genres in the third rung.
    For Option #2, I learned about the relationships that Frank has had in the music industry, which artists he has collaborated with. The lines easily guide me to each artist and shows me the albums Frank has worked on.â€‹

    Exploiting relationship visual potential
    For Option #1, each rung is notated with a Primary, Secondary, Tertiary. More importantly, the rungs help encode each genre in a hierarchy, complemented by the text. The colors, while they don't have a codified meaning to them, are used to encode classification of genres. The white lines all help to separate genres, and include several subgenres together. The rungs interact by not fusing into each other, again, distinguishing the levels apart. The lines do touch to give meaning that they are each related in some way, and the strength of the lines, help to constantly distinguish genres.
    For Option #2, this one is a bit more intuitive. Figures are all in circular shapes of the same size and color, to promote the idea that each of these entities are equal, or just musicians in this case. There is a small hierarchy, as the graph shows Frank's own collabs, so Frank becomes the peak, and everyone else is connected to him. The lines provide guidance and interaction between other artists, with text to describe the interaction.

    • Layout

    First Draft

    Option #1
    Arrangement
  • There is top-down, horizontal layout to this model. The main placement of the circular rings with "Frank Ocean" are meant to catch the viewer's attention. Starting from there, we see leading lines that lead you towards the right and towards the median block which is also red. From the median, we eventually reach the bottom sections. The viewer would be looking left to right throughout, and gradually make his/her way down the graphic. The 30 slant provides a contrast from the left side to the right side, and pushes viewers to view it left to right much more than the second one.
    • Grouping
  • The location of blocks between the median have distinguishing information displayed. The top blocks denotes Frank Ocean, such as his age, biography, accomplishments, while the bottom displays information about personnel, genres, and studio locations, things that are less central to Frank Ocean and of less direct importance. The middle median is a meeting point of both blocks; Frank Ocean's discography is associated with all of the surrounding blocks, like his biography, his accolades, and also the genres, people he's worked with, and studios.
    • Size of elements
  • Similar to the grouping, the largest blocks on top denote the history of Bauhaus, whereas the smaller blocks on the bottom display personnel, courses, and locations. This variation in size implies that the bottom blocks are complementary to the top blocks in terms of information.
